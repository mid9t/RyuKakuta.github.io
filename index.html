About

I work on machine learning systems where explanation matters as much as prediction. My focus is on failure modes, when models break, why they break, and how to surface the causes in ways that actually change decisions. That interest runs through everything I do: from model design and feature engineering to system architecture, especially where technical rigor has direct stakes in business outcomes.

Currently, I’m a Data Science undergraduate at UC Berkeley. In my recent work, I built an automated root cause analysis system for sales forecast anomalies, combining traditional models with LLM-based agents to reason over internal business data and external events. The focus wasn’t accuracy alone, but enforcing data correctness, interpretable attribution, and system reliability under real-world constraints.

Across projects, I tend to work end-to-end: cleaning and joining messy data, engineering features, selecting models for the right reasons, and building orchestration logic that holds up under unreliable inputs. I care about understanding tools at a fundamental level, that's usually where the real leverage is.

Outside of ML, I spend time thinking about systems more broadly: economics, politics, philosophy, and how complex behavior emerges from simple rules. I also like lifting, biking, and mountaineering (finished Half Dome in 2025), and have a running interest in art.
